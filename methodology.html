Methodology & Provenance

Complete documentation of data sources, index construction, econometric specification, and interpretive literature for the comparative Urban Quality Index and Human Development Index analysis of Paris and Mexico City.

I. Urban Quality Index: Construct Validity and Normalization

The Urban Quality Index (UQI) operationalizes spatial inequality of planning as a composite of publicly provisioned infrastructure and amenities at the neighborhood scale. Unlike market-derived indices (property values, rental yields), each UQI component records a state decision: where transit lines were routed, where sewers were laid, where hospitals and libraries were sited.

To construct the UQI we solve a small optimization problem. For each neighborhood i, decade t, and variable v, we define the normalized score as the solution to a min-max rescaling on the cross-sectional distribution:

n(i,t,v) = ( x(i,t,v) − minj x(j,t,v) ) / ( maxj x(j,t,v) − minj x(j,t,v) )

For variables where lower is better — crowding, density — we apply the complement: one minus that quantity. The composite UQI is then the equal-weight arithmetic mean of the selected normalized scores, scaled to 0–100.

UQI(i,t) = 100 · (1 / |Vt|) Σv n′(i,t,v)

The critical methodological choice is the normalization domain. We normalize within city and within decade — not globally — because we are measuring relative planning quality across neighborhoods of the same city at a given moment, not absolute welfare levels. A global normalization would conflate the question of whether Paris is better than Mexico City with the question of whether the 7e is better than the 19e, which are different questions the project keeps separate.

For transit access specifically, we made a design choice that is worth stating explicitly. The naive measure — metro stations per 100,000 residents — creates an endogeneity between population and the variable itself. When central arrondissements depopulate after 1970, as Paris's did, their transit score rises mechanically not because transit improved but because the denominator shrank. We replace this with stations per square kilometer of arrondissement area, a fixed geographic quantity that is immune to population dynamics. This is the same logic that motivates using land area rather than population as the denominator in density-adjusted amenity measures in the urban economics literature.

Component definitions

Transit access (Paris)
Cumulative métro stations per km² of arrondissement area by decade opening date (RATP/Wikidata). CDMX uses weighted station-equivalents per 100,000 residents (Metro = 1.0 benchmark; surface modes fractional).
Electricity, piped water, drainage
Share of inhabited dwellings with service connection, by census decade. Source: INEGI Censos Generales de Población y Vivienda (Mexico); reconstructed arrondissement-level utility rollout curves calibrated to Haussmann-era sewer completion literature (Paris). See Prefecture de Paris archival reports and Pinon (2000) on Parisian network completion.
Hospital access
Major hospital-level institutions per 100,000 residents. Excludes primary-care clinics to maintain comparability. Source: Secretaría de Salud facility registries (CDMX); AP-HP and FHF facility lists (Paris).
Green space per capita
Current public green-space inventory (m²) divided by historical population at decade t. This is a pressure indicator: it shows how per-capita amenity changed as population grew. Source: SEDEMA (CDMX); opendata.paris.fr Espaces verts (Paris).
Third spaces
Museums and public libraries per 100,000 residents, cumulative by documented opening date. Source: INAH/CONACULTA registries (CDMX); opendata.paris.fr équipements culturels (Paris).

II. Human Development Index: Sub-Indicator Construction

HDI follows the UN Human Development Report 2022 geometric aggregation procedure. For each neighborhood i:

HDIi = (Ihealth,i × Ieducation,i × Iincome,i)1/3

Mexico City

Alcaldía-level HDI values derive from the PNUD Mexico municipal Human Development Index workbook (1990, 2000, 2010, 2015, 2020). Sub-indices combine life expectancy from CONAPO/SSA mortality tables, mean and expected years of schooling from INEGI Educación, and GNI per capita from INEGI income surveys adjusted to PPP.

Paris

Arrondissement-level HDI is computed locally from INSEE Filosofi 2021 median disposable income (income index), ORS Île-de-France life expectancy (health index), and INSEE education sub-indices, aggregated with the UN HDR 2022 geometric mean. Years 2000 and 2010 are documented backcasts from the 2020 cross-section. Full specification: data/paris/hdi-methodology.md.

III. Econometric Specification: UQI–HDI Association

We estimate cross-sectional OLS regressions for each city at the 2020 observation:

HDIi = α + β · UQIi + εi

where UQIi is expressed on the 0–1 normalized scale. We report R² = 1 − SSres/SStot. Standard errors are not cluster-adjusted given the small-n context (n = 20 Paris, n = 16 CDMX); results are presented as descriptive association, not causal identification. The regression does not control for confounders (industrial legacy, topography, colonial land tenure) that co-determine both planning investment and human development.

VI. Limitations and Epistemic Boundaries

Pre-1968 Paris arrondissement population figures rely on documented historical estimates, not continuous census series at constant geography. Paris HDI education sub-indices employ income-rank proxies where arrondissement diploma tables were unavailable in provided INSEE extracts. Historical UQI values for both cities combine direct census observations with reconstructed rollout curves; they are designed for comparative visualization across time and space, not for policy benchmarking against official indices. The UQI–HDI regression establishes association, not causation. Omitted variables (industrial zoning, topography, tenure regimes, racial and class sorting) likely co-determine both planning investment and human development outcomes.
