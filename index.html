The Plan
and the Price

Mexico City and Paris were reshaped by the same 19th century planning ideals.
The question is whether that planning reached everyone equally,
or whether the map of investment became the map of who lives well.

· · ·

Has urban planning produced equal cities, or does spatial inequality of planning translate into inequality of life?

This project measures planning quality and human development across 20 Paris arrondissements and 16 Mexico City alcaldías, and asks whether neighborhoods that received more planned infrastructure also rank higher on health, education, and income.

What this project contributes

To our knowledge, Paris and Mexico City have never been compared at the neighborhood scale using a shared planning-quality index paired with Human Development Index scores. This site is an original comparative analysis: we built the Urban Quality Index (UQI), applied it identically to both cities across 150 years of census and infrastructure data, and synthesized sources that urban economics, development, and planning literatures typically treat separately. The result is a novel data synthesis, not a repackaging of existing city rankings, that makes spatial inequality of planning visible in the same visual language for two capitals that share a Haussmann-era origin but diverged in state capacity and periphery formation.

Why these two cities?

  • 1853–1870: Haussmann redraws the city by decree — 12 boulevards, 325,000 displaced
  • State-led planning has dominated since — metro, parks, housing estates, all by central authority
  • 20 arrondissements with a persistent west-to-east wealth gradient
  • Today: one of the most planned cities in the world, inequality intact
  • 1876–1910: Díaz imports the Haussmann template — Reforma, monuments, drainage
  • Post-1940: migrants outpaced the state; colonias populares built the city from below
  • 16 alcaldías with a sharp central-west vs. eastern-periphery divide
  • Today: ~40% of housing began informally, planning arrived after residents

Same planning model adopted. Different capacity to implement it. Same question: did it produce equal cities?

Our UQI index

We chose to create a custom Urban Quality Index because existing measures could not answer the specific question of our project. Property values and rental prices show how markets sort neighborhoods, but they do not directly measure long-term planning investment. Broader livability rankings often operate at the citywide scale, which makes them too general for a neighborhood-by-neighborhood comparison between Paris and Mexico City. Single indicators, such as transit access, green space, hospitals, or cultural institutions, are also insufficient on their own because urban planning is multidimensional. The legacy of 19th-century boulevard planning was not only about streets, but about how infrastructure, public space, services, and accessibility were distributed across the city.

For this reason, a standardized neighborhood-level Urban Quality Index was necessary. The UQI allows us to compare arrondissements and alcaldías on the same scale, decade by decade, using variables that capture the material outcomes of planning. Instead of treating urban development as one isolated feature, the index measures whether planned areas accumulated multiple advantages over time. This makes it possible to ask whether historical planning decisions continue to map onto present-day differences in human development.

How we compute and compare

To construct the UQI we solve a small optimization problem. For each neighborhood i, decade t, and variable v, we define the normalized score as min-max rescaling on the cross-sectional distribution within the same city and decade. Lower-is-better variables use the complement. The composite UQI is the equal-weight arithmetic mean of selected normalized scores, scaled to 0–100. Paris transit uses stations per km² (not per capita) so scores do not rise mechanically when central arrondissements depopulate.

n(i,t,v) = ( x(i,t,v) − minj x(j,t,v) ) / ( maxj x(j,t,v) − minj x(j,t,v) )

HDI follows the UN HDR 2022 geometric mean at the arrondissement or alcaldía level (2020). Full specification: methodology & sources.

To test whether planning maps onto lived outcomes, we estimate ordinary least squares for each city:

HDIi = α + β · UQIi + εi

We report the coefficient of determination R² = 1 − SSres/SStot. A higher R² indicates that a larger share of HDI variance co-moves with UQI across neighborhoods in 2020.

Correlation is not causation. But when R² is substantial and the spatial pattern persists across decades (see below), the association is not a statistical artifact. It reflects a century-long process in which planned infrastructure accumulated in particular districts and human development followed. The historical lens matters: neighborhoods that gained transit, drainage, and civic amenities first have remained at the top of both indices.

The lines do not converge. In both cities, the UQI gap between best-served and worst-served neighborhoods has remained wide for over a century. Paris's eastern arrondissements gained utilities later and never fully closed the transit and amenity gap. Mexico City's peripheral alcaldías improved after mid-century but remain structurally lower. This persistence is why the HDI correlation above is historically grounded: urban planning is not a one-time intervention. It is a cumulative, path-dependent process whose effects last generations.

Conclusion

What both cities showed

Both Paris and Mexico City concentrate planning quality in particular districts. Infrastructure, parks, transit, and civic amenities cluster in central and western neighborhoods. When human development is laid on top, the same gradient appears: neighborhoods with stronger planning scores also rank higher on health, education, and income.

How the cities differ

The spread between best and worst served neighborhoods is wider in Mexico City than in Paris. The geometries differ. Paris's gradient runs west to east through a planned center; Mexico City's follows a colonial inheritance still legible 130 years later. The trajectories differ. Paris's pattern has barely moved in fifty years; Mexico City's lower scoring alcaldías are slowly catching up. But the shared pattern is what matters for the argument.

In both cities, where you are born still shapes how long you live, how far you go in school, and what you earn. Spatial inequality of planning is spatial inequality of life.
